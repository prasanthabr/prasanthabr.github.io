# tl;dr
Our experience with a Siemens Dishwasher and why German may not be the best bet in India. Certainly Siemens with their support's sense of humour is not the path to tread.

**UPDATE**: Siemens says they will replace this machine with a brand new one by Thursday 12th Nov, with a fresh warranty. The cynic is me is waiting for the next curve ball.

**UPDATE 2**: Siemens replaced the machine yesterday 9th Nov and got around installing it the same day, a good 3 days early. This machine is made in Turkey :)


# Background
Since my parents retired, the house was becoming a beehive of visitors. Usual chores magnified by multiples depending on the number of guests who troop in for their own holidays and so my Mom decided to buy a Dishwasher. And as part of a generation who have seen German as the epitome of reliability, she decided it has to be German. And what else to select other than the 168 year old German. So Siemens it was. Siemens Dishwasher (Type / E-NR SN 26L800 IN/29  FD / C-No.No. 9409 00302). All those numbers sure make it sound very German indeed. 

Of course hearing horror stories of no warranty support from pieces bought from Flipkart and online retailers, the Dishwasher was ordered from an authorised Siemens dealer locally. And on 25th August 2015 the Siemens machine arrived. And by 19th October 2015, the Siemens had washed perhaps a total of 10 times and the Siemens was sure dead.

Not very German in terms of reliability. Congratulations, Siemens, you have disproven a myth!

# Siemens German for Reliability

Come to think of it, perhaps we should have seen what could have happened. 

Siemens is famous for its faulty wind turbines (apparently the blades broke off), the hackable industrial equipment (remember Stuxnet - yes it was Siemens), adulterated medical devices (yes there is a US FDA letter effectively barring import during 2012). And [German engineering][1] in general is getting a bad name now days - think Volkswagen, think German made Fujistu Laptops. 

One wonders whether the equipment that is sold in India is indeed models which do not have a market anywhere else. It is almost guerrilla tactics that are being used here. Very low investment in dealerships, low investment clearly in support structures (ok, sorry, we can’t fix your machine coz the parts come from Germany) sold only on the brand name at a higher than usual mark-up.
But what comes out as the best factor in the mix is the Customer Support.

#Siemens German for Customer Support - surprisingly the funniest I have ever encountered
The only contact point given for customer support is a telephone number. You can get an email id if you go dig the Siemens Website. After half a dozen contacts and calls to the support number, my Dad sent an email to Siemens. 
Ms Taira was extremely gracious. She pointed out that complaints can be raised via the telephone number (why bother us over an email)
I can count 12 mail interactions on this subject over the last 18 hours, and I almost know the Siemens response by heart. The matter has been forwarded to the concerned department. I wonder what department we were mailing in the first place.

Well, I guess now that they have indeed proven that the machine is unreliable, what are they going to do about this? (As of Nov 9th Siemens replaced and installed a new machine)


# 28th Oct - The Siemens German Troubleshooting
After constant noise, Siemens has woken up and seems to be interested in troubleshooting the issue. Now, the fact that the machine went dead within 2 months (Typo Error Corrected and Updated 28th Oct), and the fact that it has been used hardly ten times lead me to believe that this is perhaps an old piece or an issue that Siemens internally knows about.

But Siemens seems to be interested in pointing out that this is due to rodent damage. (One should understand that it would fit in perfectly and resurrect the reliability reputation that has been questioned on this post)

And so two gentlemen landed at home with a part that doesn’t match the machine that we have, to troubleshoot and fix the issue. Well, my parents wanted to know what was wrong. Of course the module (sorry, we got you the wrong part, but that’s a minor hiccup), and of course a rodent attack. My Dad put on his glasses to see the rodent bitten part - and that’s when it dawned, normal untrained people won’t be able to see the elusive rat attack. There is no physical damage. The rat has probably shorted it. Am guessing the rats trained in chip design too?

In any case, the part shall be ordered from Mumbai Siemens and shall be replaced. And now the switch and bait - the technician wants a visit report to be signed. And the report reads that the part has been damaged by a rodent. Not the most straight forward of encounters to be sure.

# 03rd Nov - Siemens promise

On 28th Sept, my dad was promised that the machine would be fixed by Monday, 2nd Nov. Joseph Antony from Siemens called up to confirm that the fix would be done by 2nd. But we get an email saying the machine would be fixed by 4th Nov - as per Joseph, the team is merely under promising, with a view to over deliver.

2nd November came and went; 3rd November I call up Joseph Antony to remind him he had mentioned this would be fixed by 2nd. Joseph explained that the part was delayed. On asked why that happened, he explained it just happened - implying this was something he couldn’t control and was a random act of God (?)

# 03rd Nov - Escalation Contact Points
Given that whatever has been promised by Joseph was falling short, I asked for the escalation contact points. Joseph says that the customer service email id is the only contact available for paying customers. I ask him whether I can quote him on the fact that Siemens refuses any other escalation contact point and he responds as if it were a no brainer - Of Course is the answer. 

#03rd Nov - The switch
In the next 30 mins my dad calls up and says he got an email from Joseph. It’s a classic! Joseph says - *as promised* Siemens will fix the issue by 4th.

#04th Nov - German troubleshooting - it’s funny again
Someone troops in with the parts, decided that he will replace the module and runs the machine. The machine dutifully runs and throws out an error. And with characteristic reassurance, the tech tells my dad that he is prepared for this and has brought in another part too. Well, this part turns out to a rusty piece. Having got tired of German quality, my dad protests. The tech is still prepared, of course this part is new, and of course he is merely testing the machine.

Knowing my dad, the classic response would have been, test all you want, but you are not putting in a rusty old part in the machine.

Anyway, long story short - tech reassembles the machine, successfully too. Leaves with his new rusty part, gets his manager Joseph on the line. Joseph says he will plan to get a replacement machine. He is told that we need a brand new machine and the same is confirmed. Considering processing time - he says that the machine would be available by Thursday next.

#05th Nov - The reconfirmation.
Jerin (I may have got his name wrong here, sorry!) calls up saying he has read my tweets and has read this blog and that as an exception, says that the machine would be replaced. 

I am very suspicious since we have been subjected to multiple switch and baits. But he insists that the delivery would be Thursday as promised by Joseph to my dad. The warranty would start from the delivery of the new machine and it would indeed be a new machine.

Well, and so we wait to see what the next curve ball would be.

#10th Nov - The support machinery pulls a rabbit from the hat
My parents received a replacement machine on 9th Nov. The local Siemens engineer had been forewarned I suppose, since he called up to check whether the machine has arrived. Delivery was smooth and the old machine was removed and shipped back on the same truck.

By evening, 10 days short of a month, there was a working machine to replace the one that went kaput on 19th Oct 2015. The local engineer says this is the first machine that he has seen breaking down within 5 years of installation. I shall a little salt with that. Christmas the crowds of guests shall land home and treat themselves to a holiday, and everyone who is family would be pushed to the edge making sure the guests are pampered. I sure hope the Siemens is up to it.


Thanks!
