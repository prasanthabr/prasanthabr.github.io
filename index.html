Salesforce PriceBooks

Nov 21st, 2014 3:44 am

What are PriceBooks

Pricebooks are a list of products and their associated prices. With each product and the price called a price book entry. PriceBooks and pricebook entries can be associated with opportunities or quotes. An organization can have 1, Standard Price (std price book) 2, List Price (custom price book) and 3, Sale Price (price associated with opportunities or quotes – need not be the price in price books)

Deleting a product or pricebook deletes the corresponding price book entry Deleting the product from a price book does not affect any other price book

Types of PriceBooks

Standard PriceBooks

Lists all products and their standard or default prices. Acts as the master list of products and prices. An organization has a single standard pricebook.

Custom PriceBooks

List of products with the list prices for market segments or customer groups containing discounted or higher prices than the standard list price. A custom pricebook entry is possible only for an active pricebook entry in a standard price book. The standard pricebook need not be active.

NOTE: To view pricebooks you need a read permission of products and pricebooks

Schedules

The payment and delivery cycles for a product or an opportunity product once a price is listed for a product.

Schedules can be:

  • Quantity Schedules - payment is made in one lot, while the delivery of products are scheduled
  • Revenue Schedules - delivery of products is in one lot, while the payment is scheduled over timeline
  • Revenue and Quantity Schedules - delivery is scheduled and payment is scheduled too – why would this be done as opposed to creating separate opportunities, so that we would have more accurate forecasting visibility and tagging to employees

NOTE: Standard schedules are helpful only if customers make payment on the same timeline or product delivery is made on a regular timeline

Blunder to Salesforce Idea Upvote

Nov 16th, 2014 8:13 am

The other day I was asked to support a client discussion call that our team was building in Salesforce.com. I would have avoided any such discussion if I could, since this was one of the four projects I was involved in and this particular one was handed out to me during the user acceptance phase since “we are short of experienced people”. This meant that I haven’t kept myself up to date on the requirements and of the way the project was progressing. Hell, there were so many revisions and time line shifts that I really wasn’t sure what the correct time line was.

Well, I think I amply demonstrated that I would rather not get into this call, but then this was client initiated and all other resources had conflicts. And so, I stepped into the spot light and I let the two groups of client users fight their respective positions without weighing in. On hind sight, this was a smart move since the final decision came back to the one that I was hoping for while all the users fury was directed against each other and not the poor consultant.

Save for one tiny detail on which the client groups were moving into a consensus area which would have meant rework and review for the implementation team. Creation of a separate field to capture the nature of the request from a web to lead form. I did not want that. I wanted to users to agree on reusing a checkbox that was already on the data model. The client was willing to consider this suggestion, but they thought this was not intuitive enough for the end user, the customer support reps who will finally use the system. The discussion again veered to a new field.

I needed to control this and end the discussion. Why not change the label to make it more intuitive. That proved the clincher, and I quickly ended the call. Now, I did make a rather smart suggestion, but then other businesses use the system too. How about changing field labels against the record type, perhaps that would work. Aaagh, so my gut feel of how things should work has failed me. Salesforce field labels can be set for the entire object and everyone sees the same label.

I thought that was a little lame (probably coz I made a lame commitment and this was standing in my way) and thought it makes sense to have unique labels for each record type, without forcing the implementation teams to use Visual Force on any issue they face.

And so I went in an up voted the idea that I thought best sums up the thought process that I had. Do have a look and see whether it makes sense. Intuitive labels for each set of records, efficient reuse of fields, lesser data model reviews, fewer client to client arguments. The idea is 7 years old and has 3320 votes, I think this change is under rated and needs to get more support.

Invictus

Out of the night that covers me,
Black as the pit from pole to pole,
I thank whatever gods may be
For my unconquerable soul.

In the fell clutch of circumstance
I have not winced nor cried aloud.
Under the bludgeonings of chance
My head is bloody, but unbowed.

Beyond this place of wrath and tears
Looms but the Horror of the shade,
And yet the menace of the years
Finds and shall find me unafraid.

It matters not how strait the gate,
How charged with punishments the scroll,
I am the master of my fate,
I am the captain of my soul.

Ulysses

Nov 2nd, 2014 6:41 pm

It little profits that an idle king,
By this still hearth, among these barren crags,
Match’d with an aged wife, I mete and dole
Unequal laws unto a savage race,
That hoard, and sleep, and feed, and know not me.
I cannot rest from travel: I will drink
Life to the lees: All times I have enjoy’d
Greatly, have suffer’d greatly, both with those
That loved me, and alone, on shore, and when
Thro’ scudding drifts the rainy Hyades
Vext the dim sea: I am become a name;
For always roaming with a hungry heart
Much have I seen and known; cities of men
And manners, climates, councils, governments,
Myself not least, but honour’d of them all;
And drunk delight of battle with my peers,
Far on the ringing plains of windy Troy.
I am a part of all that I have met;
Yet all experience is an arch wherethro’
Gleams that untravell’d world whose margin fades
For ever and forever when I move.
How dull it is to pause, to make an end,
To rust unburnish’d, not to shine in use!
As tho’ to breathe were life! Life piled on life
Were all too little, and of one to me
Little remains: but every hour is saved
From that eternal silence, something more,
A bringer of new things; and vile it were
For some three suns to store and hoard myself,
And this gray spirit yearning in desire
To follow knowledge like a sinking star,
Beyond the utmost bound of human thought.

This is my son, mine own Telemachus,
To whom I leave the sceptre and the isle,—
Well-loved of me, discerning to fulfil
This labour, by slow prudence to make mild
A rugged people, and thro’ soft degrees
Subdue them to the useful and the good.
Most blameless is he, centred in the sphere
Of common duties, decent not to fail
In offices of tenderness, and pay
Meet adoration to my household gods,
When I am gone. He works his work, I mine.

There lies the port; the vessel puffs her sail:
There gloom the dark, broad seas. My mariners,
Souls that have toil’d, and wrought, and thought with me—
That ever with a frolic welcome took
The thunder and the sunshine, and opposed
Free hearts, free foreheads—you and I are old;
Old age hath yet his honour and his toil;
Death closes all: but something ere the end,
Some work of noble note, may yet be done,
Not unbecoming men that strove with Gods.
The lights begin to twinkle from the rocks:
The long day wanes: the slow moon climbs: the deep
Moans round with many voices. Come, my friends,
’T is not too late to seek a newer world.
Push off, and sitting well in order smite
The sounding furrows; for my purpose holds
To sail beyond the sunset, and the baths
Of all the western stars, until I die.
It may be that the gulfs will wash us down:
It may be we shall touch the Happy Isles,
And see the great Achilles, whom we knew.
Tho’ much is taken, much abides; and tho’
We are not now that strength which in old days
Moved earth and heaven, that which we are, we are;
One equal temper of heroic hearts,
Made weak by time and fate, but strong in will
To strive, to seek, to find, and not to yield.

Converting Salesforce ID From 15digit to 18digit

Jul 17th, 2014 12:52 am

understanding & leveraging record ids

A salesforce.com id field is an auto generated 15 character base-62 id consisting of 26 lower case characters (a-z), 26 upper case characters (A-Z) and 10 numerals (0-9) that remains unique within the organisation data and constant throughout the lifetime of the record, analogous to a key value in a relational database. The first three characters of the id refer to the object to which the record belongs. The remaining 12 characters are said to identify the record id, while the first character of this 12 reference the salesforce.com pod on which the record is created.

It is now well documented that while this system works well inside the salesforce.com ecosystem, salesforce.com admins face issues on oft used third party tools due to lack of support for case sensitivity. Salesforce.com has responded with a case insensitive 18 character id to resolve the situation and have recently made available a CASESAFEID (Id) function for easy conversion of case sensitive ids. While API calls return an 18 character id and salesforce.com recommends using the 18 character id, the salesforce.com reports continue to return a 15 digit id. Attempts have also been made to create workarounds to make third party tools case sensitive.

While these fixes and solutions work in many situations, for the average salesforce.com admin the process remains fraught with complexity. Usage of reports is more common than the api supported dataloader, due to a premium on code footprint and field count, the casesafeid function is seldom used, leaving admins to get creative when working with data.

A common solution is to use the case sensitive vlookup on a csv file. The solution hinges on a case sensitive comparison using the EXACT function in MS Excel, which is prone to deliver a no match found verdict if the raw data is unsorted and hence may be unreliable. Of course, lot of developers have developed solutions in JavaScript, VBA and Apex to solve the issue. The excel connector bestows a custom function that can easily convert a 15 digit id to an 18 digit one.

The idea itself behind converting the case sensitive id to a case insensitive one is quite simple. The 15 digit id is appended with three extra characters which would identify the position of cases in the strings. Though commonly mistaken as a checksum, the three characters are not checksums since these three characters are not unique to the id and hence would not be able to identify an id corruption.

Though there does not seem to exist an official documentation on the conversion, the algorithm is commonly available. The solution calls for slicing the 15 digit id to three parts. Based on the location of upper cases in these slices, a 5 digit binary code is generated. The generated binary code picks up a matching character from the 62 count character list, appending the three generated characters to the original 15 character id.

This may make life that much more easier for the average admin who is forced to extract CSV reports for comparion, by generating copy files with converted 18 character ids instead of the 15 digit characters generated using the standard Salesforce.com reports.
